The AI Landscape — and Where You Stand

Before touching a single model, you need a map. This one place gives you a mental grid for every buzzword on the roadmap — and shows why AI Engineer is a real job that needs far less math than you might fear.

Your win

By the end you can (1) place any AI term — ML, deep learning, LLM, AGI — on a single map, and (2) explain how the AI Engineer differs from the ML Engineer and the Researcher, and why your build-first, math-light path is the right one for this role.

1The four terms are nested dolls, not rivals

The single biggest source of confusion in AI is treating AI, machine learning, deep learning, and generative AI as competing things. They're not. Each sits inside the previous one.1

ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE
MACHINE LEARNING
DEEP LEARNING
GENERATIVE AI · LLMs
  • AI — any technique that makes a machine act "intelligently," even a hand-written rulebook (a chess engine, a spam filter).
  • Machine learning — AI that learns patterns from data instead of being told the rules by hand.2
  • Deep learning — ML using many-layered neural networks that discover useful features on their own.
  • Generative AI — deep learning that produces new content. The text branch is the Large Language Model (LLM) — Claude, GPT, Gemini. This is the layer you'll live in.
Don't confuse these two neighbours

Data Science extracts insight from data (overlaps ML, but aims at understanding, not shipping a product). Data Engineering builds the pipelines and storage that feed data to everything above. Useful to recognise; not your destination.

2"Narrow" vs "General" — and where generative fits

A second axis describes how broad an AI's competence is:

  • Narrow AI (ANI) — good at one task or a narrow band. Everything that exists today is narrow — yes, including the most impressive LLMs.
  • General AI (AGI) — human-level competence across any task. Hypothetical; not achieved.

The key trap: "generative" is a capability, not a level. A model can generate poetry and code and still be narrow. So AI ≠ AGI — when the news says "AI," it almost always means a narrow, generative system.1

3The three jobs — and why yours needs less math

"Works in AI" hides three very different careers. The distinction decides what you must learn.3

RoleCore workMathBuilds…
AI Engineer ← you Build products on top of existing, pretrained models. Light Apps: prompting, RAG, agents, APIs
ML Engineer Train, deploy, and optimise custom models. Medium–heavy Models + pipelines (MLOps)
AI Researcher Invent new architectures and methods. Heavy New knowledge (papers)

Here's the punchline for your mission: the heavy math — calculus, linear algebra, statistics — lives inside creating and training a model. That's the researcher's and ML engineer's world. The AI Engineer builds on models that are already trained, so the job is mostly software: choosing a model, prompting it well, feeding it the right data (RAG), giving it tools (agents), and making the system reliable, cheap, and fast.4 That's why math-light is not a shortcut here — it's the correct shape of the role.

The mental model to keep

Researchers invent the engine. ML Engineers build and tune engines. You build the car people actually drive.

Recall (say it out loud first): why does an AI Engineer need less math than an ML Engineer?

Because the AI Engineer builds on models that are already trained. The heavy math lives inside creating the model — the ML Engineer's and researcher's job. Your craft is software: prompting, retrieval, tools, evaluation, and system design.
