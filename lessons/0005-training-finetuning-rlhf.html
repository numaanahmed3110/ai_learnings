From Raw Predictor to Helpful Assistant

In Lesson 02 we said the assistant you chat with is a "tamed" next-token predictor. This lesson opens that box. You'll see the exact three-stage pipeline that turns a pile of internet text into Claude or ChatGPT — and why each stage exists.

Your win

You'll be able to walk through the whole training pipeline — pre-training → supervised fine-tuning → RLHF — explaining what each stage feeds in, what it produces, and every buzzword you'll hear (foundation model, SFT, reward model, PPO, DPO, alignment). You'll also understand why models are simultaneously so knowledgeable and so confidently wrong.

1The big picture: three stages

A production LLM isn't trained in one shot. It's built up in three distinct stages, each solving a problem the previous one couldn't. Here's the whole map before we zoom into each box — keep coming back to this diagram.

2Stage 1 — Pre-training: where the knowledge comes from

Pre-training is the giant, expensive stage that gives the model its raw intelligence. The recipe is almost absurdly simple: take an enormous amount of text — web pages, books, code, articles — and train the model to do the one thing from Lesson 02: predict the next token. Do that across trillions of tokens, and the model is forced to absorb grammar, facts, writing styles, and reasoning patterns, because all of those help it guess the next token more accurately.1

The key buzzword here is self-supervised learning. Normally, "supervised" training needs humans to label data ("this email = spam"). Pre-training needs zero human labels, because the label is already sitting in the text: the correct "next token" is simply the word that actually came next. The data labels itself. That's the trick that let these models scale — there's an ocean of unlabeled text, and it's all free training signal.

CS analogy

Think of pre-training like building a massive read-only cache of the internet's patterns, compressed into the model's weights. It isn't storing pages verbatim (it's not a database) — it's storing the statistical shape of language and knowledge, the way a lossy compressor keeps the gist.

How does the model actually "learn" from a wrong guess? Through a loss function — a single number scoring how wrong it was — and a process that nudges the model's internal weights to make that number smaller next time. You don't need the math, but seeing the idea once makes everything click:

The pre-training objective (the gist)

loss = −log P(the token that actually came next)

What it means: if the model gave the real next token a high probability, −log P is small (good); if it gave it a tiny probability, the loss is large (bad). It's a surprise score. Why it's used: it converts "was the guess good?" into a number you can automatically push downward. Where: computed on every training example, in pre-training and fine-tuning. Analogy: like a golf score — lower is better, and each round (batch of text) the model tweaks its "swing" (weights) to shave a bit off. This tweaking is gradient descent, a Phase-3 topic we'll keep purely intuitive.

The output of all this is a base model (also called a foundation model). It's brilliant at continuing text but socially clueless: ask a base model "What is the capital of France?" and it might reply with a list of more quiz questions, because on the internet that string is often followed by… more quiz questions. It has knowledge but no instinct to help. That's the problem Stage 2 fixes.

3Stage 2 — Supervised fine-tuning: teaching manners

Fine-tuning means taking an already-trained model and training it a bit more on a new, targeted dataset — nudging its existing weights toward a new behaviour without starting over. Supervised fine-tuning (SFT), also called instruction tuning, does exactly this using a curated set of examples that look like the interaction we want:

What one SFT example looks like

Instruction: "Explain photosynthesis to a 10-year-old."
Ideal response: "Photosynthesis is how plants make their own food using sunlight…" (written by a human labeler)

Show the model tens of thousands of these high-quality demonstrations and it learns the format and habit of being a helpful assistant: read an instruction, produce a direct, useful answer. Note what SFT does and doesn't do — it mostly teaches behaviour and style, not brand-new facts (the facts came from pre-training). The result is an instruct model: it follows instructions, but it doesn't yet have a fine sense of which of two decent answers is better, or where the safety lines are. Humans are much better at judging answers than at writing the perfect one every time — and that insight is the whole reason for Stage 3.

4Stage 3 — RLHF: learning from human preferences

RLHF stands for Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback, and it's the alignment stage — where the model is shaped to be helpful, honest, and harmless, and to match the tone we want. It works in two moves.1

Why the two-step dance? Because human feedback is slow and expensive. Instead of a human grading millions of answers, humans grade a sample, that taste is captured in the reward model, and then the LLM can practise against the reward model millions of times with no human in the loop. It's a clever way to scale human judgement.

Ties back to Phase 4 (Reinforcement Learning)

RLHF borrows RL's vocabulary, which you'll meet properly later. The LLM is the policy (the thing choosing actions — here, tokens), the reward model provides the reward, and PPO is the update rule. If those words are fuzzy now, that's fine — just register that RLHF is where the roadmap's "agent / reward / policy" ideas first show up in the LLM story.

One modern footnote you'll hear: DPO (Direct Preference Optimization). It reaches a similar destination as RLHF but skips building a separate reward model and the fiddly RL loop, learning directly from the preference pairs. Many recent models use DPO or its cousins because it's simpler and more stable. For now: RLHF and DPO are two roads to the same goal — aligning the model to human preference.

5Why this matters to you as an AI Engineer

You will almost never pre-train (that's a big-lab, big-budget job), and you'll fine-tune only occasionally. So why learn this? Because the pipeline explains the behaviour you'll build around every day:

  • Why models are confidently wrong. Truth was never the training target — plausibility was (Stage 1). Alignment reduces hallucination but can't delete it.
  • Why models have a "personality" and refuse some requests. That's RLHF/alignment (Stage 3) doing its job.
  • Why prompting works. SFT trained the model to take instructions seriously — so how you phrase them genuinely steers it.

And it sets up a decision you'll make constantly: to give a model new information or behaviour, do you fine-tune it, or use RAG (feed the info in at question-time)? A first-pass rule of thumb, which we'll unpack in a later lesson:

Fine-tuning

  • Best for behaviour, format, style, tone.
  • Bakes changes into the weights (permanent, needs retraining to update).
  • Costs training effort + data prep.

RAG

  • Best for knowledge / facts, especially fresh or private data.
  • Info supplied at inference in the context window — easy to update.
  • No training; it's an engineering/retrieval problem (your wheelhouse).

6Buzzword recap

Every term from this lesson, in one line each — if any is still fuzzy, that's a perfect question to ask me.

Pre-training — the big self-supervised stage that gives the model its knowledge by predicting next tokens over massive text.
Self-supervised — no human labels needed; the next token in the real text is the label.
Base / foundation model — the pre-training output; knowledgeable but only autocompletes, doesn't follow instructions.
Fine-tuning — extra training of an existing model on a targeted dataset to shift its behaviour.
SFT / instruction tuning — fine-tuning on human (instruction → answer) demos to make an assistant that obeys.
RLHF — Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback; aligns the model using human preference rankings.
Reward model — a model trained to predict which answers humans prefer, i.e. to score answers.
PPO — Proximal Policy Optimization; the RL algorithm that nudges the LLM toward higher-reward answers.
DPO — Direct Preference Optimization; a simpler modern alternative to the RLHF loop.
Alignment — making the model's behaviour match human intent and values (helpful, honest, harmless).
