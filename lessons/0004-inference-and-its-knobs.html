Inference & the Knobs You Control

Training builds the model; inference is running it to get output — and it's the part you actually touch. A handful of settings change how the model behaves. Knowing them is the difference between fighting the model and steering it.

Your win

You'll know the core inference settings — temperature, top-p, max tokens, stop sequences — what each does, and which to reach for when. This is your first real "control panel" as an AI Engineer.

1Inference vs training

Training is the expensive, one-time process of building the model (the ML Engineer's and researcher's world). Inference is every time you use the finished model to generate a response.1 As an AI Engineer you live almost entirely in inference — usually one API call at a time.

2Where the knobs act

Recall from Lesson 02: at each step the model produces a probability for every possible next token. Something has to pick one. That picking step — called sampling — is exactly what the knobs tune.2

3The settings that matter

Temperature — the randomness dial

The single most important knob. It controls how much the model gambles on lower-probability tokens.2

TemperatureBehaviourUse for
Low (0–0.3)Focused, consistent, near-deterministicExtraction, classification, code, factual answers
Medium (0.4–0.7)BalancedGeneral chat, explanations
High (0.8–1.2)Varied, creative, surprisingBrainstorming, fiction, marketing copy

Rule of thumb: want one right answer → go low; want many different ideas → go high.

Top-p (nucleus sampling) — the other randomness dial

Instead of a temperature, top-p says "only consider the most likely tokens whose probabilities add up to p" (e.g. 0.9). It's an alternative way to control variety. Tune temperature or top-p, not both at once — changing both makes results hard to reason about.2

Max tokens — the length cap

The maximum number of tokens the model may generate in its reply. It guards your cost and latency — but set it too low and the answer gets cut off mid-sentence. Remember (Lesson 03): output tokens also count against the context window.

Stop sequences — the off-switch

One or more strings that, when generated, make the model stop immediately. Handy when you want output to end at a marker (e.g. stop at \n\n or END) rather than run on.

You'll also meet

Frequency and presence penalties — small dials that discourage the model from repeating itself or push it toward new topics. Nice to recognise; not worth memorising yet.

4The one gotcha

"Temperature 0" is not a guarantee

Low temperature makes output much more consistent, but even at 0 you can't always assume byte-identical results across calls — hardware and model-serving details introduce tiny variability. Design as if outputs may vary; don't hard-code exact string matches on model output.
